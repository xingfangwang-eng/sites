**Module 6: Insurance Tactics Deep Dive**

### Exposing 5 Common Insurance Company Tactics to Deny or Reduce Claims

Insurance companies often employ various tactics to deny or reduce claims. Being aware of these tactics can help victims navigate the claims process more effectively.

1. **Delaying Payment:** Insurance companies may delay payment by claiming that the victim has not provided sufficient information or that the claim is still under investigation.

Countermeasure: Victims should keep a record of all correspondence with the insurance company, including dates, times, and details of conversations.

2. **Undervaluing Claims:** Insurance companies may undervalue claims by using lowball estimates or denying coverage for certain damages.

Countermeasure: Victims should obtain multiple estimates from independent repair shops and keep detailed records of all damages.

3. **Denying Coverage:** Insurance companies may deny coverage by claiming that the victim was not wearing a seatbelt or that the vehicle was not properly maintained.

Countermeasure: Victims should keep a record of all maintenance records and ensure that they were wearing a seatbelt at the time of the accident.

4. **Using Fine Print:** Insurance companies may use fine print to deny coverage or reduce claims.

Countermeasure: Victims should carefully read their insurance policy and ask questions if they are unsure about any terms or conditions.

5. **Using Third-Party Vendors:** Insurance companies may use third-party vendors to review claims, which can lead to biased or incomplete assessments.

Countermeasure: Victims should request that the insurance company use their own in-house adjusters or third-party vendors that are independent of the insurance company.

**Module 9: Expanded FAQ**

### Frequently Asked Questions with Legal Basis

1. **Q:** What is the statute of limitations for filing a claim in Florida?
**A:** The statute of limitations for filing a claim in Florida is typically 4 years, but can vary depending on the specific circumstances of the case. (Florida Statutes § 95.11)

2. **Q:** Can I sue the trucking company if the driver was responsible for the accident?
**A:** Yes, you may be able to sue the trucking company if the driver was responsible for the accident. The trucking company may be liable under a theory of vicarious liability. (Florida Statutes § 768.25)

3. **Q:** What is the difference between compensatory and punitive damages?
**A:** Compensatory damages are intended to compensate the victim for actual losses, such as medical expenses and lost wages. Punitive damages, on the other hand, are intended to punish the defendant for reckless or wanton misconduct and deter similar behavior in the future. (Florida Statutes § 768.72)

4. **Q:** Can I pursue a claim if I was a passenger in the vehicle?
**A:** Yes, you may be able to pursue a claim if you were a passenger in the vehicle. The passenger may have a claim against the driver or the trucking company for negligence or reckless behavior. (Florida Statutes § 768.25)

5. **Q:** What is the role of the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA) in investigating commercial trucking accidents?
**A:** The FMCSA is responsible for investigating commercial trucking accidents to ensure compliance with federal regulations and to identify safety hazards. (49 CFR § 390.5)

6. **Q:** Can I sue the manufacturer of the truck if it was defective?
**A:** Yes, you may be able to sue the manufacturer of the truck if it was defective. The manufacturer may be liable under a theory of products liability. (Florida Statutes § 768.25)

7. **Q:** What is the difference between a settlement and a verdict?
**A:** A settlement is an agreement between the parties to resolve the claim for a certain amount of money. A verdict, on the other hand, is a determination by a judge or jury of the amount of damages owed to the victim. (Florida Statutes § 768.72)

8. **Q:** Can I pursue a claim if I was a pedestrian or bicyclist?
**A:** Yes, you may be able to pursue a claim if you were a pedestrian or bicyclist. The pedestrian or bicyclist may have a claim against the driver or the trucking company for negligence or reckless behavior. (Florida Statutes § 768.25)

9. **Q:** What is the role of the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) in investigating commercial trucking accidents?
**A:** The NHTSA is responsible for investigating commercial trucking accidents to identify safety hazards and develop regulations to prevent future accidents. (49 CFR § 390.5)

10. **Q:** Can I sue the government if the accident was caused by a government vehicle or employee?
**A:** Yes, you may be able to sue the government if the accident was caused by a government vehicle or employee. The government may be liable under the Federal Tort Claims Act. (28 U.S.C. § 1346)
