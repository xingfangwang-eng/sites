**Module 4: Punitive Damages Analysis (400 words)**

In California, punitive damages are available in cases where the defendant's conduct is found to be "oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious." (California Civil Code § 3294). To establish punitive damages, the plaintiff must demonstrate that the defendant's conduct was "willful, wanton, or reckless" and that the defendant "knew or should have known" of the risk of harm.

In this scenario, if the plaintiff can establish that the truck driver's failure to perform a daily inspection, as required by 49 CFR § 396.17, was willful or reckless, and that the driver knew or should have known of the risk of harm, punitive damages may be available.

The court will consider the following factors in determining the amount of punitive damages:

| Factor | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Defendant's wealth | The defendant's financial resources and ability to pay punitive damages |
| Degree of reprehensibility | The severity of the defendant's conduct and the harm caused to the plaintiff |
| Ratio of punitive to compensatory damages | The ratio of punitive damages to compensatory damages awarded to the plaintiff |

In this scenario, if the plaintiff is awarded $1 million in compensatory damages, the court may consider awarding punitive damages in an amount up to 4:1, resulting in a total award of $4 million.

    *   Insurance company tactic: Prolonging the claims process to avoid paying out benefits.
    *   Countermeasure: Keep detailed records of all correspondence with the insurance company, including dates, times, and the content of discussions.
2.  **Denying Liability:**

    *   Insurance company tactic: Disputing their client's responsibility for the accident.
    *   Countermeasure: Collect and preserve evidence, including police reports, witness statements, and physical damage to the vehicle.
3.  **Undervaluing Claims:**

    *   Insurance company tactic: Misrepresenting the value of the claim or undervaluing damages.
    *   Countermeasure: Consult with a qualified appraiser to determine the actual value of damages, including property damage, medical expenses, and lost wages.
4.  **Citing Pre-Existing Conditions:**

    *   Insurance company tactic: Arguing that the victim's pre-existing conditions contributed to their injuries.
    *   Countermeasure: Consult with a medical expert to determine the extent to which pre-existing conditions may have contributed to the victim's injuries.
5.  **Making Misleading Statements:**

    *   Insurance company tactic: Making false or misleading statements to the victim or their attorney.
    *   Countermeasure: Record all conversations with the insurance company, including dates, times, and the content of discussions.
