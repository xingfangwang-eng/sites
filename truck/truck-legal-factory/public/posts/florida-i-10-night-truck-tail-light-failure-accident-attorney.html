Punitive damages are awarded to punish the defendant for their egregious conduct. In this case, the truck driver's failure to maintain the rear lighting system and perform regular inspections of his vehicle constitutes egregious conduct. The plaintiff is entitled to punitive damages under Florida law.

To determine the amount of punitive damages, we must consider the following factors:

* The defendant's wealth: The truck driver's income and assets. * The severity of the plaintiff's injuries: The plaintiff's medical bills, lost wages, and pain and suffering. * The defendant's culpability: The truck driver's level of negligence and intent.

Under Florida law, punitive damages are capped at three times the amount of compensatory damages. However, in cases where the defendant's conduct is found to be particularly egregious, the court may award punitive damages in excess of the cap.

Module 8: State-Specific Nuances

In Florida, truck accident cases are governed by the Florida Statutes and the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Regulations (FMCSRs). Here are some state-specific nuances to consider:

1. Florida's Comparative Negligence Law:

Florida follows a comparative negligence law, which means that the plaintiff's damages may be reduced if they are found to be partially responsible for the accident.

2. Florida's Punitive Damages Law:

Florida allows punitive damages in cases where the defendant's conduct is found to be egregious. However, the amount of punitive damages is capped at three times the amount of compensatory damages.

3. Florida's Truck Accident Statute of Limitations:

The statute of limitations for truck accident cases in Florida is four years from the date of the accident.
