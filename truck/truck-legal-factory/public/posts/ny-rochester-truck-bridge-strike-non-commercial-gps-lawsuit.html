Module 6: Insurance Tactics Deep Dive

Insurance companies often employ various tactics to deny or reduce claims in truck accident lawsuits. Some common tactics include:

1. Delaying the Claim

Insurance companies may delay the claim by requesting unnecessary information or documentation, thereby slowing down the processing of the claim.

Countermeasure: Keep detailed records of all correspondence with the insurance company, including dates, times, and contents of conversations.

2. Denying Liability

Insurance companies may deny liability by claiming that the accident was caused by the plaintiff's actions, rather than the defendant's.

Countermeasure: Gather evidence to prove the defendant's liability, including witness statements, photographs, and police reports.

3. Undervaluing Damages

Insurance companies may undervalue damages by using lowball settlement offers or by claiming that the plaintiff's injuries are not as severe as claimed.

Countermeasure: Work with an attorney to calculate damages accurately, including medical expenses, lost wages, and pain and suffering.

4. Using Pre-Existing Conditions as a Defense

Insurance companies may use pre-existing conditions as a defense to reduce or deny liability.

Countermeasure: Gather evidence to prove that the pre-existing condition did not contribute to the accident or the plaintiff's injuries.

5. Using the "Comparative Fault" Defense

Insurance companies may use the "comparative fault" defense to reduce liability by claiming that the plaintiff contributed to the accident.

Countermeasure: Gather evidence to prove that the plaintiff did not contribute to the accident, including witness statements and physical evidence.

Module 7: Evidence Collection Checklist

Surviving a truck accident can be a traumatic experience, and collecting evidence can be a daunting task. Here is a checklist of evidence to collect:

1. Photographs

* Take photographs of the accident scene, including the damaged vehicles and any debris. * Take photographs of the injured parties, including any visible injuries.

2. Witness Statements

* Gather witness statements from anyone who witnessed the accident, including drivers, passengers, and pedestrians. * Ask witnesses to provide a written statement or to be interviewed by an attorney.

3. Police Reports

* Obtain a copy of the police report, including the accident report and any witness statements. * Review the police report for accuracy and completeness.

4. Medical Records

* Gather medical records from all healthcare providers who treated the injured parties, including hospital records, doctor's notes, and medical bills.

5. Lost Wages

* Gather documentation of lost wages, including pay stubs, W-2 forms, and any other relevant financial records.

6. Property Damage

* Gather documentation of property damage, including repair estimates and receipts for repairs.

7. Police Video

* Obtain a copy of any police video or dashcam footage that may have captured the accident.

Module 9: Expanded FAQ

Here are some additional FAQs:

1. Q: What is the statute of limitations for filing a truck accident lawsuit in New York State?

A: The statute of limitations for filing a truck accident lawsuit in New York State is 3 years from the date of the accident.

Q: What is the difference between a "negligent" and a "reckless" act in a truck accident lawsuit?

A: A "negligent" act is one that falls below the standard of care expected of a reasonable person. A "reckless" act is one that shows a conscious disregard for the rights and safety of others.

Q: Can I sue the trucking company for damages if the truck driver was at fault?

A: Yes, you can sue the trucking company for damages if the truck driver was at fault. The trucking company may be liable for damages under the doctrine of respondeat superior.

Q: What is the difference between "compensatory" and "punitive" damages in a truck accident lawsuit?

A: Compensatory damages are intended to compensate the injured party for their losses, including medical expenses, lost wages, and pain and suffering. Punitive damages are intended to punish the defendant for their wrongdoing and to deter similar behavior in the future.

Q: Can I use the "comparative fault" defense to reduce liability in a truck accident lawsuit?

A: Yes, you can use the "comparative fault" defense to reduce liability in a truck accident lawsuit. However, this defense is typically used by the defendant to reduce liability, not by the plaintiff.

Q: What is the role of the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA) in a truck accident lawsuit?

A: The FMCSA is responsible for regulating the trucking industry and ensuring that truck drivers comply with federal regulations. In a truck accident lawsuit, the FMCSA may be involved in investigating the accident and determining whether the truck driver complied with federal regulations.
