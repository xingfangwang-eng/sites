Legal Statutes

In the US, truck drivers are subject to various federal and state regulations that govern their behavior on the road. The Federal Motor Carrier Safety Regulations (FMCSA) require commercial truck drivers to:

* Check their blind spots regularly (49 CFR 392.82) * Maintain a safe distance from other vehicles (49 CFR 392.83) * Avoid changing lanes without checking for other vehicles (49 CFR 392.84)

Additionally, state traffic laws often impose similar requirements on commercial truck drivers. For example, California's Vehicle Code Section 22450 states that "no person shall drive a vehicle upon a highway at such a slow speed as to impede or block the normal and reasonable flow of traffic, except when reduced speed is necessary for safe operation, because of a grade, or in compliance with law."

Insurance Company Tactics

Insurance companies often employ various tactics to exploit victims of truck blind spot collisions. Some common strategies include:

* Denying liability or minimizing the extent of the damages * Offering lowball settlements to pressure victims into accepting a quick payout * Using complex jargon and technical terms to confuse and intimidate victims * Failing to disclose important information or hiding relevant evidence

It's essential to be aware of these tactics and to seek professional representation to protect your rights and interests.

5 Professional FAQs Q1: What are the most common injuries sustained in truck blind spot collision cases?

A1: Common injuries in truck blind spot collision cases include lacerations, broken bones, head trauma, and whiplash. The severity of the injuries can vary depending on the impact and the individual's circumstances.

Q2: How do insurance companies determine the value of a truck blind spot collision claim?

A2: Insurance companies typically assess the value of a truck blind spot collision claim by considering factors such as the extent of the damages, the cost of medical treatment, the impact on the victim's daily life, and the potential for future complications or disabilities.

Q3: Can I still recover damages if I was partially responsible for the accident?

A3: Yes, you can still recover damages if you were partially responsible for the accident, but the amount of compensation you receive will be reduced by the percentage of fault attributed to you. For example, if you were 20% responsible for the accident, you would be entitled to 80% of the total damages.

Q4: How long do I have to file a claim for a truck blind spot collision?

A4: The statute of limitations for filing a claim for a truck blind spot collision varies by state. In most cases, you have two to three years from the date of the accident to file a claim. However, it's essential to consult with an attorney as soon as possible to ensure you meet the deadline and preserve your rights.

Q5: Can I pursue a claim against the truck driver or the trucking company?

A5: Yes, you can pursue a claim against both the truck driver and the trucking company. The trucking company may be liable for the driver's actions under the doctrine of respondeat superior, which holds an employer responsible for the actions of its employees.
