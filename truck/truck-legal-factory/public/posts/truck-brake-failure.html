Legal Statutes

The Federal Motor Carrier Safety Regulations (FMCSR) govern the safe operation of commercial vehicles, including trucks. According to 49 CFR § 393.41, trucks must be equipped with a functioning air brake system, and drivers must maintain their vehicles in good working condition. Additionally, the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA) requires trucking companies to implement safety procedures, such as regular inspections and maintenance, to prevent accidents.

In the event of a truck brake failure, victims may be entitled to compensation under various state and federal laws. For example, the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) governs the liability of trucking companies for damages caused by their vehicles. Under the UCC, trucking companies may be held liable for damages, including medical expenses, lost wages, and property damage.

Insurance Company Tactics

Insurance companies often employ tactics to minimize payouts and shift the blame onto victims. Some common tactics include:

* Denying or downplaying the extent of injuries * Questioning the validity of medical expenses * Attempting to discredit witnesses or experts * Offering low-ball settlements or delaying payment * Failing to disclose relevant information or policies

Victims must be aware of these tactics and take steps to protect themselves. This includes:

* Keeping detailed records of medical expenses, lost wages, and property damage * Gathering witness statements and expert opinions * Consulting with an attorney to ensure their rights are protected * Negotiating with insurance companies in a fair and transparent manner

5 Professional FAQs

1. Q: What are the most common causes of truck brake failure? A: The most common causes of truck brake failure include worn or damaged brake pads, faulty brake fluid, and inadequate maintenance.

2. Q: How do I prove that a truck's brake failure was the cause of my injuries? A: To prove that a truck's brake failure was the cause of your injuries, you'll need to gather evidence, including witness statements, expert opinions, and documentation of the accident scene.

3. Q: Can I file a lawsuit against the trucking company if their driver was not at fault? A: Yes, you may be able to file a lawsuit against the trucking company if their driver was not at fault. This is known as a vicarious liability claim.

4. Q: How long do I have to file a lawsuit against the trucking company? A: The time limit for filing a lawsuit varies by state, but it's typically between 1-3 years from the date of the accident.

5. Q: Can I negotiate with the insurance company directly, or do I need an attorney? A: While you can negotiate with the insurance company directly, it's highly recommended that you consult with an attorney to ensure your rights are protected and you receive fair compensation.
